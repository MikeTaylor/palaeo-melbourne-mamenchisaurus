### Tom Rich

The cast of _Mamenchisaurus_ was mounted upright in the former location of Museums Victoria. At the new location which opened in 2000, there was not a ceiling high enough to mount it that way. So at present, the cast is mounted with the neck close to horizontal. Even when mounted upright, in order to do so, one cervical vertebra was set aside. From memory, Eugene Gaffney saw the _Mamenchisaurus_ cast upright before the _Barosaurus_ specimen was mounted upright at the AMNH on 3 December 1991. 

It was my suggestion that the cast of the skeleton of _Mamenchisaurus_ be mounted upright. This was done after I had observed that the acetabulum of the pelvis was thicker where the femur articulated with it when the skeleton was upright than when horizontal. Museums Victoria acquired the cast of the skeleton of _Mamenchisaurus_ as a result of a traveling exhibition there called Dinosaurs from China that was held at Museums Victoria in the early 1980s. As part of that agreement, moulds were made of the skeletons of _Mamenchisaurus_ and _Tsintaosaurus_. The moulding and casting of the skeletons was done jointly by technicians from Museums Victoria and the Institute of Vertebrate Palaeontology and Paleoanthropology. The cast of the skeleton of _Mamenchisaurus_ was originally mounted upright when it became available so that was done long before 3 December 1991.

This message is being copied to Peter Swinkels who participated in the moulding and casting of the skeleton of Mamenchisaurus.


### Stephen Poropat

Mike, I woke up early, saw your email and thought: Tom must have written about this somewhere. 

And indeed I just found a passage written by Tom where he states that the Melbourne Museum _Mamenchisaurus_ was mounted in a rearing position in 1989.

It appears in the book _The Artist and the Scientists_ (published in 2010, with exemplary artist and scientist Peter Trusler as lead author, Pat Vickers-Rich as second author, and Tom last; each section has the author[s] indicated), on pages 66-67 (no photo though - I think the Museum prohibited photography at the time, I certainly have no photos of the galleries as they were back then):

> Wondering if it was plausible for the _Mamenchisaurus_ to stand on its hind legs with its neck upstretched, as a few artists had previously rendered restorations of sauropods [SP: Greg Paul's rearing _Mamenchisaurus_ vs. _Yangchuanosaurus_ from his 1988 _Predatory Dinosaurs of the World_ comes to mind], I examined its pelvis. What particularly struck me was that part of the articular surface where the femur would contact it when the animal stood upright was thicker than the surface of contact when the animal stood on its four feet. This made me think that the pelvis was capable of withstanding a greater stress when the animal was upright, and thus it was indeed plausible for _Mamenchisaurus_ to stand in that way. So, in 1989 it was remounted in the McCoy gallery [SP: of Melbourne Museum, then in the State Library of Victoria] in that pose — a stance which has since been used for other sauropod skeletons in museums around the globe."

Hope this helps! This does indicate that it pre-dates the AMNH _Barosaurus_ mount (at least in terms of date of completion; not sure when AMNH started the ball rolling with their mount).

Also, so you know: I would have started going to Melbourne Museum with my family in 1989 or 1990 (when I was 4-6 years old). My earliest memories of the Museum include seeing that sauropod there, rearing against a tree (some kind of palm I think).

Thanks for the link [to the Baromount manuscript] - a quick skim of the first few pages does suggest that some degree of narrative change might be in order!

I'm not sure what rationale underpinned the prohibition of photography. There must be photos out there, either in the Museum's own archives or in those of the State Library. When I am in Melbourne next (might be later this year) I might be able to do some digging.
